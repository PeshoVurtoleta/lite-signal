PROTOCOL CHANGE vs the 1.2.0 run (read this first). These numbers come from the
one-engine-per-cold-process protocol (run-all-bench.sh: FW=<engine> per child,
medianed by aggregate.mjs). The prior 1.2.0 table ran several engines in one
process, which is unsound: each engine has its own ReactiveNode/signal shape, so
sharing the drive()/set() call sites degrades their inline caches monomorphic ->
megamorphic, and nursery-allocating engines borrow a prior engine's warm heap.
Both effects move numbers by tens of percent. The isolated protocol below is the
correct comparison; the 1.2.0 single-process numbers (in git history) should be
treated as superseded. The engine hot paths are byte-identical 1.2.0 -> 1.2.2 --
this is a measurement correction, not a performance change.

deltaheap = transient heap allocated during the timed loop (GC pressure).
retained  = heap surviving a forced GC after the loop (leaks / live graph).
sink      = anti-DCE Float64Array validated non-zero after the loop.

WHY THIS DIFFERS FROM THE 1.2.0 SINGLE-PROCESS TABLE (and why it's the truer story):
  The shapes where lite now wins BIG are the allocation-heavy ones -- the dynamic
  DAGs (read order flips every iteration, dense fan-in) where alien churns the
  nursery. Run in isolation, alien pays the full GC cost of that churn and lite's
  zero-allocation pool pulls 31-48% ahead. In the old single-process run, alien
  borrowed lite's already-warm heap and those gaps looked far smaller (DYNAMIC DAG
  read +8.8% then, +44% now). Conversely the cheap stable shapes (KAIROS,
  BROADCAST, wide app/dense) barely allocate, so isolation removed a small
  cross-contamination tailwind and alien now edges them by 4-7% -- inside host
  noise on this 10-year-old machine.

  The honest summary: lite-signal's advantage is precisely where its zero-GC
  design pays off -- high-churn dynamic graphs and wide fan-in (MUX +35%). On the
  cheap, low-allocation stable shapes it runs at parity with alien (+/-7%). The
  one structural loss is the 256-deep pipeline (DEEP CHAIN), where alien's flatter
  representation wins on long propagation paths.

deltaheap (transient allocation) -- the zero-GC story is UNCHANGED:
  - On stable + wide shapes lite-signal allocates ~0.3KB across 20,000 iterations
    (hot paths reuse pool slots). On MUX: lite ~0.3KB vs alien-signals ~7,800KB
    and preact ~12,500KB in the same loop.
  - On the chaotic DYNAMIC/SELECTIVE DAG shapes even lite-signal allocates 9-13MB
    (retracking re-links on a genuine dependency-set change) -- still far less
    than alien-signals (39-42MB on the same shapes), which is the mechanism behind
    lite's isolated win there.
  - KAIROS lite retained ~71KB: the live 1002-node graph pinned in the pre-
    allocated pool. The pool IS the working memory.

retained heap:
  - Negative values mean V8 compacted below the pre-bench baseline during the
    post-loop forceGc() -- no leak. alien-signals' allocation is GC pressure, not
    a leak.

Architectural reading:
  - lite-signal leads on fan-in (MUX) and the dynamic/selective DAGs -- the high-
    churn and aggregation patterns that dominate dashboards, scoreboards, and HUDs
    under live input -- and remains the only zero-deltaheap library on every
    stable scenario.
  - alien-signals leads on the 256-deep pipeline (DEEP CHAIN) and runs at parity
    on the cheap stable shapes. The retracking machinery (version-stamped O(1)
    reconciliation + markEpoch clean-read short-circuit, shipped 1.1.4) is what
    keeps lite ahead on the dynamic DAGs while shipping zero allocation.
  - 1.2.0 -> 1.2.2 changed no hot path. The 1.2 owner tree, pre-batch revert, and
    aggregated flush errors are paid at construction / batch-close / throw time,
    not in steady-state reads. The table moves here are the measurement-protocol
    correction described at the top, not engine changes.
