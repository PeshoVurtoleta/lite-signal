PREALLOC / PROTOCOL NOTE (read this first). These numbers are the default-config run
(prealloc: "eager" -- pools constructed up front, the zero-allocation-in-hot-path
contract). They come from the one-engine-per-cold-process protocol (run-all-bench.sh:
FW=<engine> per child, medianed by aggregate.mjs): each engine has its own
ReactiveNode/signal shape, so sharing call sites across engines in one process would
degrade their inline caches monomorphic -> megamorphic and let nursery-allocating
engines borrow a prior engine's warm heap. The isolated protocol below is the correct
comparison. The engine hot paths are byte-identical 1.2.2 -> 1.3.0-rc -- 1.3.0 adds
growable/lazy pools, an intrusive mark stack, and a small config surface, all paid at
construction / pool-growth time, not in steady-state reads -- so these track the 1.2.2
numbers; the deltas are run-to-run noise on this old host, not engine changes.
(prealloc: "lazy" trades a little first-construction allocation on the dynamic shapes
for a smaller resident heap and faster cold start; steady state is identical.)

deltaheap = transient heap allocated during the timed loop (GC pressure). Reported
            below as the AVERAGE delta-heap across reps -- on this 10-year-old host a
            single rep's GC timing can inflate the average, so the near-zero shapes
            read as small non-zero averages; their median floor is the ~0.3KB
            pool-reuse value (true zero-GC steady state).
retained  = heap surviving a forced GC after the loop (leaks / live graph).
            Structurally unchanged from 1.2.2 (same graph, same pool); not separately
            re-measured this cycle.
sink      = anti-DCE Float64Array validated non-zero after the loop.

WHY THE WINS CLUSTER WHERE THEY DO:
  The shapes where lite wins BIG are the allocation-heavy ones -- the dynamic DAGs
  (read order flips every iteration, dense fan-in) where alien churns the nursery.
  Run in isolation, alien pays the full GC cost of that churn and lite's
  zero-allocation pool pulls 29-48% ahead. The cheap stable shapes (KAIROS, large
  app, wide dense) barely allocate, so there is no GC tailwind for the pool to
  exploit and the two engines land within a few percent -- inside host noise on this
  10-year-old machine.

  The honest summary: lite-signal's advantage is precisely where its zero-GC design
  pays off -- high-churn dynamic graphs and wide fan-in (MUX +38%). On the cheap,
  low-allocation stable shapes it runs at parity with alien (+/-4%). The two losses
  are BROADCAST (-10%, pure fan-out) and the 256-deep pipeline (DEEP CHAIN -18%),
  where alien's flatter representation wins on long propagation paths.

deltaheap (transient allocation, average across reps) -- the zero-GC story holds:
  - lite-signal wins 8 of 9 scenarios on transient heap. On the truly stable app
    shapes (LARGE WEB APP, WIDE DENSE, SMALL SELECTIVE) lite allocates ~0.3KB across
    the run (hot paths reuse pool slots) vs alien's 41KB / 11.6MB / 23.9MB.
  - On MUX: lite ~38KB vs alien-signals ~3,926KB and preact ~238KB in the same loop
    (the ~38KB is the across-reps average; the median is the ~0.3KB pool-reuse floor).
  - On the chaotic DYNAMIC/SELECTIVE DAG shapes lite-signal allocates 1.5-5.1MB
    (genuine retracking re-links on a real dependency-set change) -- still far less
    than alien-signals (24-37MB on the same shapes), which is the mechanism behind
    lite's isolated win there.
  - The one heap loss is BROADCAST: lite ~72KB vs alien ~21KB -- a pure fan-out with
    no retracking, where alien's flatter representation touches less transient memory.

retained heap:
  - Negative values mean V8 compacted below the pre-bench baseline during the
    post-loop forceGc() -- no leak anywhere. alien-signals' allocation is GC
    pressure, not a leak. Retained heap is structurally unchanged from 1.2.2: the
    pre-allocated pool IS the working memory, and 1.3.0 does not alter the live-graph
    shape (under eager the pool is sized up front; under lazy it grows to the same
    in-use set).

Architectural reading:
  - lite-signal leads on fan-in (MUX) and the dynamic/selective DAGs -- the high-
    churn and aggregation patterns that dominate dashboards, scoreboards, and HUDs
    under live input -- and remains the only zero-deltaheap library on every truly
    stable scenario.
  - alien-signals edges BROADCAST and the 256-deep pipeline (DEEP CHAIN) and runs at
    parity on the cheap stable shapes. The retracking machinery (version-stamped O(1)
    reconciliation + markEpoch clean-read short-circuit, shipped 1.1.4) is what keeps
    lite ahead on the dynamic DAGs while shipping zero allocation.
  - 1.2.2 -> 1.3.0 changed no hot path. The 1.3.0 growable/lazy pools, intrusive
    mark stack, and config surface are paid at construction / pool-growth time, not
    in steady-state reads. The table moves here are run-to-run host noise, not engine
    changes.
